# Facilitator Plan: 2-Hour AWWA ICS-WATER Session

## Session goal

Teach the core idea of Cyber Realm ICS-WATER, explain the water-sector cyber scenario, and give players enough time to play the Tier 1 Small Org vs Script Kiddie prototype.

## Audience assumption

Players may include water operators, utility leaders, IT staff, OT staff, security professionals, and people with little card-game experience.

Teach this as a tabletop exercise with cards, not as a complex trading card game.

## Total time

2 hours.

## Recommended agenda

| Time | Activity | Goal |
|---:|---|---|
| 0-10 min | Welcome and story setup | Explain why water-sector cyber resilience matters |
| 10-20 min | Explain the two teams | Blue protects operations; Red follows the attack chain |
| 20-30 min | Explain card anatomy | Realm, card type, lifecycle symbol, cost, points, text |
| 30-40 min | Guided sample turn | Show one Red action and one Blue response |
| 40-70 min | Play Round 1 guided | Players learn by playing with facilitator help |
| 70-80 min | Debrief Round 1 | What happened? What did the attack chain look like? |
| 80-105 min | Play Round 2 faster | Let players make more independent decisions |
| 105-115 min | Debrief Round 2 | Discuss water-sector lessons and security takeaways |
| 115-120 min | Closing | Ask what was fun, confusing, useful, and realistic |

## Opening script

Cyber Realm ICS-WATER is a water-sector cybersecurity tabletop exercise disguised as a trading card game.

The Red Team is trying to move through the Cyber Kill Chain: recon, weaponize, delivery, exploit, install, command and control, and actions on objectives.

The Blue Team is trying to keep the utility running by detecting, responding, recovering, and reinforcing.

The goal is not just to win. The goal is to understand how cyber events can affect water operations and how practical defenses reduce impact.

## Simple explanation for players

Blue Team protects:

- Well
- Water Tower
- Pump Station
- Chlorination Unit
- RTU or PLC
- Gateway
- Operator Laptop
- SCADA Web Interface

Red Team attacks using:

- Recon
- Phishing
- Credential attacks
- Public exploits
- Malware
- Command and control
- Operational impact

## Teaching rule

Do not explain every card before play starts. Explain only what players need to begin.

Players should learn the rest through guided play.

## Card explanation order

1. Card name tells the story.
2. Card type tells what it does.
3. Realm color tells the domain.
4. Lifecycle symbol tells the training stage.
5. Cost tells what it takes to play.
6. Attack or defense points tell impact or resilience.
7. Game text tells the special rule.
8. Learning note explains the real-world lesson.

## Guided sample turn

Use this example before the first round:

1. Red Team plays NMAP.
2. Facilitator explains: This is reconnaissance. The attacker is looking for exposed services.
3. Blue Team reveals Gateway or SCADA Web Interface.
4. Blue Team plays Port Closure if available.
5. Facilitator explains: This is response and reinforcement. The defender is reducing exposed services.

Then start the game.

## First-round facilitator style

During Round 1, the facilitator should help players openly.

Use prompts like:

- What asset do you think is most important to protect?
- What does this attack stage represent?
- Do you have a Defense card that can respond?
- What would this look like in a real utility?
- What would you do after this incident in real life?

## Second-round facilitator style

During Round 2, let players decide more independently.

Only step in when:

- Rules are unclear
- A player is stuck
- A cyber term needs explanation
- A water-sector lesson should be highlighted

## Recommended win condition for the demo

Use a scenario-based win condition instead of only reducing SCP to zero.

Scenario: Water Tower Overflow

- Red Team wins if Water Tower and Gateway are both damaged before turn 6.
- Blue Team wins if Water Tower survives through turn 6.

This keeps the game focused on operational impact.

## Debrief questions

Ask after each game:

- What did Red Team do first?
- Which asset felt most important?
- Which defense helped the most?
- Did the attack feel realistic?
- What would you do differently at a real utility?
- What card taught you something?
- What card was confusing?
- Did this feel like training, a game, or both?

## Success criteria

The session is successful if players can explain:

- Why exposed services matter
- Why MFA and port closure help
- Why operator laptops and gateways are important
- Why backups, field technicians, and vendor support matter
- How a cyber event can become an operational water-sector problem

## Design target

The first session should feel easy to start, useful to discuss, and fun enough that players want another round.

Do not aim for perfect competitive balance in the first demo. Aim for understanding, engagement, and useful discussion.
